In 1988, I set out to create the ultimate mobility solution for fast-paced chases: the Rocket-Powered Roller Skates. Forget running. Forget flying. Why not combine the speed of a rocket with the sleek agility of roller skates? With this invention, I’d be able to keep pace with the Road Runner without worrying about overexertion, gravity, or inconvenient things like terrain.

The initial tests were surprisingly successful. When I strapped on the skates, I was blasting through the desert at over 300 miles per hour, dodging rocks, cactus, and everything else in my path. It felt like I was skating on the edge of the future, gliding effortlessly across the desert while the wind howled past me. At first, I thought I had made a mistake. Surely, this couldn’t be real—it was too perfect. A couple of practice runs later, I was unstoppable. I was even able to shave hours off the pursuit time! Who needed a car or a plane when you had rocket-powered skates?

But there was one thing I hadn’t anticipated—stopping. Sure, it was fun to accelerate to near-light speed, but when it was time to slow down, the brakes were... let’s say underdeveloped. On my first stop, I tried to gently ease into a quick halt, only to end up skidding uncontrollably across a canyon. The rocket boosters were so powerful that I soared into the air like a comet, narrowly avoiding a direct crash into a mountain. Sure, there was some overheating and the occasional wild spinout, but I was still skating like a champion.

Eventually, I realized that the secret to mastering the skates was to never stop moving. Why would I need to? After all, speed is my ally. As long as I kept the rockets firing, I could maintain a steady, stable course, all while the desert whipped by. When I finally faced the Road Runner, I was able to outpace him, not because of some high-tech gadget or explosive trap, but simply through superior roller-skating technique. Sure, he zigged when I zagged, but I was just too fast.

As for the overheating issues? Well, that’s where I learned the power of adaptation. I quickly realized that a towel wrapped around the exhaust pipe was the perfect way to keep the rockets from overheating. With a few other simple modifications, I had solved the stability issues entirely. From then on, it was nothing but smooth sailing—except when I was being chased by rocks or crashing into cacti, but hey, that’s all part of the fun!

Despite the occasional fiery exhaust bursts and launching into the air on unexpected bumps, the Rocket-Powered Roller Skates turned out to be a revolutionary success—though I can’t say the project lasted long. In fact, after a brief but intense period of trial and error (about two months, to be exact), I decided it was best to archive the project before I accidentally broke every bone in my body. The speed, however, was unmatched, and I still look back fondly on the short but thrilling time I spent roller-skating at astronomical speeds across the desert.
