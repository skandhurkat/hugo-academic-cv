As a pioneering engineer, I’ve spent years developing some of the most sophisticated explosive devices for one singular purpose: catching the Road Runner. However, after countless attempts and spectacular failures, it seems time for a retrospective on why my explosives always seem to malfunction.

First, let's talk about **the explosive launcher**. While it looks fantastic on paper—precisely calibrated trajectories, high explosive payloads, and remote detonation capabilities—the real-world application has been… less than ideal. The issue arises from a simple fact: timing is everything. I often find that my fuses are too long, and by the time the explosive reaches its target, I'm usually miles away (and often the one being blasted instead).

The **spring-loaded TNT barrels**, while effective in theory, also have their problems. The acceleration of the barrel's spring mechanism often results in a misfire, which is typically followed by an accidental detonation. There's also the small matter of environmental factors—like wind and weather—that can affect trajectory. As a result, I'm frequently caught in the blast radius, and the Road Runner is none the wiser.

Perhaps one of the most egregious examples of explosive failure came with my **rocket-powered roller skates**. After a successful launch from a cliffside, the first part of the mission went flawlessly. But the rocket engines, in their infinite wisdom, decided to overheat and explode mid-flight. I found myself plummeting down the canyon, once again defeated by an overly ambitious engineering choice.

Despite these failures, I remain undeterred. Every explosion is an opportunity to learn. While my explosives may never catch the Road Runner, they’ve certainly contributed to the broader field of destructive technology. So, here's to the next big idea. Maybe the **Acme Explosive Lunchbox** will be the one that works...
